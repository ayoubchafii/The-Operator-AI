# $100M Money Models — Reference

Source: Alex Hormozi, *$100M Money Models: How To Make Money* (Acquisition.com Vol. III)

The job of this book: design the *sequence* of offers so the business funds its own growth. Offers gets you a buyer. Money Models get the most money out of every buyer in the right order.

Where it sits in the trilogy:
- $100M Offers → *what* should I sell?
- $100M Leads → *how* do I find the people?
- $100M Money Models → *how* do I get them to buy more, faster, and forever?

## What A Money Model Is

A Money Model is a series of offers — sequenced — designed to increase three things:
1. How many customers you get
2. How much each one pays
3. How fast they pay it

The minimum bar for a good Money Model: the business makes more profit from a customer than it costs to acquire and service that customer **in the first 30 days**.

The bar for a $100M Money Model: the business makes more profit from *one* customer than it costs to get and service *many* customers in the first 30 days. At that point, cash stops being a constraint and the business can scale without limit.

This is the same idea as Client Financed Acquisition from $100M Leads, applied as a structural design principle, not just an ad metric.

## The Compounding Effect

Three levers compound multiplicatively, not additively:
- Make customers 2× more valuable
- Get 2× as many of them
- Get them 2× as fast

Result: 2 × 2 × 2 = 8× faster business growth. Triple each, you get 27×. Money Models exist to push all three at once.

## The Four Offer Types

Every Money Model is built from four offer types:

1. **Attraction Offers** — turn strangers into customers
2. **Upsell Offers** — get customers to spend more cash
3. **Downsell Offers** — turn nos into yeses
4. **Continuity Offers** — keep them paying

You can use one, two, or all four. The strongest businesses run all four in sequence.

Build order recommendation:
1. Get customers reliably (Attraction)
2. Make them pay for themselves reliably (Upsell)
3. Make them pay for *other* customers reliably (Upsell + Downsell)
4. Maximize long-term value per customer (Continuity)

## Section II — Attraction Offers (5 types)

Attraction Offers turn advertising into money by offering something free or at a discount, then making money on the back end. The mechanism: a free or discounted hook gets vastly more attention than a normal offer, then the structure recovers cost and creates profit.

### 1. Win Your Money Back
You set a goal for the customer. If they hit it, they get their money back (often as store credit). They pay now with a chance to "win it back" later.

Three structures:
- **Result-based:** lose Z lbs, make $X, hit Y customers — they bet on themselves
- **Action-based:** complete the steps you assigned (better when results are out of your control)
- **Both** combined

Why it works: customers commit cash, get more invested, get better results, refer more, and a meaningful percentage don't actually claim the refund. Use it only when refund/redemption rate is low enough to remain profitable.

### 2. Giveaways
Advertise a chance to win a big prize in exchange for contact info. After the winner is picked, offer everyone else the same prize (or related stuff) at a discount. The "everyone else" sale is where the money is — the giveaway is the lead engine.

### 3. Decoy Offer
Advertise a free or discounted offer. When the lead engages to learn more, present a *premium* offer alongside it with more features, more bonuses, stronger guarantees. Some take the cheap version, some take the premium — both pay for the funnel.

### 4. Buy X, Get Y Free
Bundle a paid item with one or more free items. The free stuff increases perceived value massively while the paid item covers cost. The more free items (and the higher their perceived value), the more units of the paid item move.

### 5. Pay Less Now or Pay More Later
Give the buyer a choice: full-price later, or discounted-price-plus-bonuses if they commit today. Same outcome, two paths. Most pick "now" — which solves your cash flow.

## Section III — Upsell Offers (4 types)

Upsells fail when:
- You offer something they don't want (too unrelated, doesn't solve their actual problem)
- You offer it at the wrong time (before they've felt the next problem)
- You offer it the wrong way (they don't believe you)

Upsells work when they offer:
- **More** of what they just got (quantity)
- **Better** version (quality)
- **New / complementary** thing (different)

### 1. The Classic Upsell — "You Can't Have X Without Y"
The moment a customer buys, their core offer creates a *new* problem. The classic upsell is the immediate solution to that next problem.

Examples: car → insurance / gas / late checkout. Coat storage → ear muff storage. Gym membership → personal training. Software → onboarding / setup.

Why it's first: easiest, most profitable, most universal. Current customers already trust you and they self-upsell when timing is right. Prerequisite: you know your customer's *next* problem better than they do.

### 2. Menu Upsell — Unsell, Prescribe, A/B, Card on File
Four tactics in sequence:

- **Unsell:** tell them what they *don't* need. Cross things off the list. Builds trust and goodwill — and when you cross out competitors' default add-ons, your remaining suggestions look uniquely tailored.
- **Prescribe:** tell them what they *do* need, *as if they already have it*. Explain how to use it, how it integrates with what they bought, and how to maximize value. Removes the "should I or shouldn't I" question entirely.
- **A/B:** ask preference, not yes/no. "Would you rather do this in the morning or after work?" Either choice is a yes.
- **Card on File:** "Want to use the card on file?" Removes the friction of pulling out a wallet.

Best when you have multiple offers solving overlapping problems.

### 3. Anchor Upsell
Show the *most expensive* thing first. If they balk, drop down to a much cheaper but still acceptable alternative. The cheap option now feels reasonable and like a deal. Two side bonuses: the high-priced anchor sometimes sells, and the cheaper option converts much better than it would have shown alone.

Best when the cheaper offer has the same core deliverable as the expensive one.

### 4. Rollover Upsell
Credit some or all of a previous purchase toward the next, bigger offer. "You already spent $500 — I'll roll that toward a full year." Removes the sting of "starting over" and re-frames the upgrade as a discount. Powerful for converting trials, short-term packages, and small purchases into long commitments.

## Section IV — Downsell Offers (3 types)

Downsells turn nos into yeses without dropping the price *just because*.

Critical principles:
- Don't drop price to rescue a sale — that's discounting, not downselling. It corrupts pricing and customers talk.
- Offer the same things in *new ways*. You're not building 100 products, you're building 100 ways to offer the same one.
- The offer is never just "the same stuff cheaper."

### 1. Payment Plan Downsells (how they pay)
They want it but can't pay full upfront. You break the payment into installments without dropping the price. "Half now, half on the first." "Three payments." "What can you do?" — work down the ladder until you find a structure that works for them.

This produces a *sequence* of offers in a single conversation. The author's process can present up to nine offers in one downsell flow before exhausting options.

### 2. Trial With Penalty (how they pay)
They start free but commit to a penalty if they don't meet your terms. They get the result, you get a customer; they don't, you still get paid. Filters out tire kickers and aligns incentives.

### 3. Feature Downsells (what they get)
Lower the price by lowering what they get. Smaller quantity, lower quality, no guarantee, no extras. "If you're okay skipping the guarantee, I can take $400 off." Works because the customer feels in control of the trade.

## Section V — Continuity Offers (3 types)

Continuity = ongoing value for ongoing payment until cancellation. The math is the unlock:

- $1,000 one-time → 10% of 100 buy → $10,000 once, $0 over time
- $50/month equivalent → 40% of 100 buy → $2,000 now + $40,000 over time

Plus 4× more customers to upsell later.

Limits: continuity isn't a great standalone Attraction Offer because it leaves you cash-poor today. Use Attraction/Upsell/Downsell to capture cash *now*, then add continuity to compound long-term.

Use continuity only where ongoing value is real. Paying forever for a one-day workshop is wrong; paying monthly for ongoing coaching, software, supplements, content, or service is right.

### 1. Continuity Bonus Offers
"Sign up today and get this bonus." The bonus is typically worth more than the first month's payment. Works because the buyer's first decision feels net-positive on day one.

### 2. Continuity Discount Offers
Give them free time — either now (first month free) or later (free month after X months). Lowers the perceived initial cost; the longer-form versions also reward retention.

### 3. Waived Fee Offer
Charge an upfront startup fee as part of joining a month-to-month plan. Then offer to waive the entire fee if they commit to a longer term. If they cancel inside the term, the fee applies. Result: high commitment, low intuitive sting.

## How To Build Your Money Model (Sequencing)

You build it in this order, one stage at a time. Don't add the next layer until the current one is reliable.

1. **Get customers reliably** — Attraction Offer working at acceptable CAC
2. **Make them pay for themselves in 30 days** — add an Upsell that pushes first-30-day revenue above (CAC + cost-to-fulfill). This is the Client Financed Acquisition threshold.
3. **Make them pay for *other* customers** — strengthen Upsells / add Downsells until first-30-day profit funds *additional* customer acquisition. Now ad spend self-multiplies.
4. **Maximize long-term value** — add Continuity to compound revenue from the existing base.

At each stage, the test is the same: does this layer make the next $1 of ad spend more profitable, faster?

## Operating Principles From The Book

- A business is its money model. "This won't work for my business" is the wrong question — replace it with "how do I make this work for my business."
- Hard-selling is for weak products. If they say no, find someone who says yes.
- Customers who pay get better results. Don't soften pricing to rescue weak conviction.
- Offers are sequenced, not stacked at random. The right offer at the wrong time is still a no.
- Combine: Attraction gets the customer, Upsell maximizes the first transaction, Downsell rescues the no, Continuity compounds the lifetime.
- Speed of cash matters as much as size of cash. 30-day payback is the threshold; faster than that is the unlock.
- Refunds happen. Give them back without drama and move on; the business model is the leverage, not the friction.

## Diagnosis Questions

When reviewing a money model, walk these:
- What is the Attraction Offer? Is the front-end free or discounted enough to dramatically lower first-touch friction?
- What is the immediate Upsell? Is it solving the *next* problem the customer just bought into?
- What is the Downsell when they say no? Is it the same product offered in a new way — or am I just discounting?
- What is the Continuity layer? Is the ongoing value real?
- Does first-30-day revenue cover (CAC + cost-to-fulfill)?
- If yes, by how much? That multiple is how fast you can scale.
- Where in the sequence is cash leaking — at acquisition, at the upsell moment, at retention?
