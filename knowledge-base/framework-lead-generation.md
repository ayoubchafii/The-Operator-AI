# $100M Leads — Reference

Source: Alex Hormozi, *$100M Leads: How to Get Strangers To Want To Buy Your Stuff* (Acquisition.com Vol. II, 2023)

The job of this book: get more strangers to *show interest* in what you sell — predictably, repeatedly, and at scale.

## Core Definition: Lead vs Engaged Lead

A **lead** is anyone you can contact. An email on a list is a lead. A name in your phone is a lead.

An **engaged lead** is someone who has shown interest in what you sell. That is the actual output of advertising — not impressions, not reach, not clicks. Engaged leads.

Most people optimize for the wrong number. Optimize for engaged leads.

## What Drives Engagement: Offers + Lead Magnets

Two things make a lead engage with an ad: a great core offer, or a great lead magnet.

A **core offer** is the main thing you sell. Going straight for the sale is the shortest path to money — try it first.

A **lead magnet** is a *complete solution to a narrow problem*, given for free or at low cost, that:
1. Is genuinely valuable enough to charge for
2. Once consumed, reveals a *bigger* problem your core offer solves

The pretzels-at-a-bar logic: salty pretzels solve hunger, then create thirst, which the bar sells. Lead magnet solves problem A, surfaces problem B, your core offer solves problem B.

A person who pays you with their *time* is more likely to pay with their *money*.

### Seven steps to build a lead magnet
1. Pick the problem to solve and who you solve it for
2. Figure out how to solve it
3. Decide how to deliver it (PDF, video, mini-course, software tool, free trial, free sample, audit, etc.)
4. Test what to name it
5. Make it easy to consume
6. Make it actually good
7. Make it easy to tell you they want more (clear CTA at the end)

A great lead magnet is itself a Grand Slam Offer. The fact that it's free does not lower the bar.

## The Core Four: The Only Four Ways To Advertise

Every way of letting a person know about your stuff sits in one of four boxes. Two audience types, two communication styles:

|                | 1-to-1 (private) | 1-to-many (public) |
|----------------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Warm** (people who know you) | Warm Outreach | Posting Content |
| **Cold** (people who don't) | Cold Outreach | Paid Ads |

That's it. If you aren't getting enough leads, you are doing one or more of these without enough skill or volume. Pick based on speed, cost, and current bottleneck — not on which one feels comfortable.

## #1 — Warm Outreach (cheapest, fastest first leads)

One-to-one contact with people who already know you. Calls, texts, DMs, emails, voice notes. Best place to get your first 5 clients, re-engage old buyers, or launch a new product line.

### The 10 steps
1. **Get your list.** Phone contacts + every email address book + all social followers. You already have hundreds. Add them up.
2. **Pick a platform.** The one with the most contacts. You'll get to the others later.
3. **Personalize the greeting.** Reference something real about them.
4. **Reach out — 100 per day.** Up to three follow-ups, once per day, until they reply.
5. **Warm them up using ACA.** When they reply: **A**cknowledge what they said, **C**ompliment them, **A**sk another question that leads toward your space.
6. **Invite their friends.** Many will say "I don't need this, but my friend might."
7. **Make them the easiest offer in the world.** Free trial, free month, free audit — the lowest possible barrier. Use the value equation to design it.
8. **Start at the top.** Reach out first to people most likely to say yes.
9. **Start charging.** After ~5 free clients have used it, switch on pricing.
10. **Keep your list warm.** Stay in the contact rotation forever — old contacts re-engage.

The ACA framework also works in any cold context. Memorize it.

## #2 — Posting Content (highest leverage long-term)

Content that grows an audience does one job: **rewards the consumer**. To reward, you must Hook, Retain, Reward.

### The Content Unit (Hook — Retain — Reward)
- **Hook attention** — get them to *notice* the content. Measured by % who start consuming.
- **Retain attention** — get them to *consume* the whole thing. Measured by % who finish.
- **Reward attention** — *satisfy* the reason they consumed it. Measured by what they do after (like, save, share, comment, follow, click, buy).

The smallest content unit can be a single tweet, image, or sentence. Hook, Retain, and Reward can happen at once.

### Topic sources (where ideas come from)
1. **Far Past** — old life lessons connected to your work. "Give the story without the scar."
2. **Recent Past** — meetings, conversations, calls from the last week. Mine your calendar.
3. **Present** — capture ideas the moment they hit. Public capture beats private notes; you also get signal on what resonates.
4. **Trending** — what is in the air right now. Tie your expertise to it.
5. **Manufactured** — set up a situation, then talk about it (an experiment, a challenge, a public test).

### Headlines
Five times more people read the headline than the body. Test, in this order: **headline > image > subheadline**. Same content, better headline, can 2-10x results.

### Format matching
Match the look-and-feel of content people already enjoy on the platform. Better-looking content out-hooks even better-quality content.

### Free Goodwill
Adding "if this helped, share it / save it / send it to one person who needs it" to good content compounds reach. Goodwill is currency.

## #3 — Cold Outreach (fast, scalable, lower trust)

One-to-one contact with strangers. Calls, emails, DMs, texts. Two things make cold work:

### a) Personalize — make the cold message look warm
Reference 1-3 specific facts about them. Compliment something. Show how it benefited you. People give time to people who already know something about them. The opening line is what wins or loses the message.

### b) Big Fast Value
Strangers give you under 30 seconds. Don't tickle interest — *blow their mind* in those 30 seconds. The lead magnet or offer must be *clearly* and *immediately* valuable. Mediocre value gets ignored.

### Volume
Cold response rates are low. Use automation, but the message must still feel personal. Volume is the unlock — see Rule of 100.

## #4 — Paid Ads (most scalable; needs unit economics)

Paid ads are the only Core Four method where you can buy your way to volume the moment money is in the account. They also punish bad business models faster than any other channel.

### The metric that matters: LTGP:CAC
- **LTGP** = Lifetime Gross Profit per customer (all revenue from that customer minus cost to deliver). Hormozi prefers LTGP over LTV because it accounts for delivery cost.
- **CAC** = Cost to Acquire a Customer.
- **Target ratio: 3:1 or better.** Below 3:1, businesses struggle to scale. At 3:1+, they take off.

### Diagnosis: ads problem or business model problem?
- If your CAC is *more than 3x* the industry average → fix the **ads** (creative, targeting, offer).
- If your CAC is *below 3x* the industry average → fix the **business model** (raise LTGP through better offer, upsells, retention, margin).

Most "my ads aren't working" complaints are actually weak business models. Costs can only approach zero; revenue per customer can grow forever. Past a point, raise LTGP, not lower CAC.

### Client Financed Acquisition (CFA)
If a customer pays you more than (CAC + cost-to-fulfill) **within 30 days**, you can scale on a credit card with no cash flow constraint. This is the holy grail of paid ads. Engineer your offer and money model so first-30-day revenue covers acquisition + delivery. Then you scale infinitely with zero cash drag.

### Ad creative priority order
Test, in order:
1. **Hook / opening 3 seconds** — biggest single lever
2. **Headline / claim**
3. **Image or video creative**
4. **Body copy**
5. **CTA**
6. **Audience / targeting** (last — most ad platforms target well by default now)

## Core Four On Steroids: More, Better, New (Rule of 100)

Once a Core Four channel is working at any level, three levers boost it:

- **More** — increase the volume of what's already working
- **Better** — improve the quality of each unit (better hook, tighter copy, sharper offer)
- **New** — same channel, new platform, new geography, new audience segment

### The Rule of 100
Do **100 primary actions per day, for 100 days straight**, on whichever Core Four method you're working.

- Warm outreach → 100 reach-outs/day
- Posting content → 100 minutes/day creating, at least 1 piece released/day
- Cold outreach → 100 reach-outs/day
- Paid ads → 100 minutes/day on ads, run them for 100 consecutive days

Most "this isn't working" judgments are made before the volume bar is even hit. Hit the bar, then judge.

## Lead Getters: Other People Who Get Leads For You

The Core Four are things *you* do. Lead Getters are *people who do them for you*. This is where leverage compounds. The four types:

### #1 Customer Referrals (Word of Mouth)
Customers tell other people. Highest trust source of new business.

Build referrals by giving value first (deliver hard, surprise upgrades, follow up, send personal notes, celebrate their wins, anticipate their next problem). Then ask. Seven ways to ask:

1. Direct ask after a result
2. Ask during onboarding (set the expectation early)
3. Trade — referral in exchange for a bonus or upgrade
4. Public ask via email or content
5. Ask to introduce a specific person
6. Ask for a review (which functions as a referral at scale)
7. Build referral into the offer itself ("bring a friend free")

Track referral rate and churn rate. Improving either compounds.

### #2 Employees
Employees do the Core Four at scale on your behalf. The chapter's own example: cold outreach revenue doubled not by changing the message or offer, but by fixing the bottleneck in *hiring* — group screenings instead of 1:1, hire faster than churn. The hidden lead constraint is often headcount.

### #3 Agencies
External companies you pay to run a Core Four channel. Best when: (a) the channel is well-understood and (b) you don't yet have internal expertise. Risks: misalignment, learning leakage, dependency. Use them to bootstrap, then either bring in-house or partner long-term with the rare ones who actually perform.

### #4 Affiliates and Partners
Other businesses with audiences who promote your stuff for a cut. Cheapest at scale because they're paid on performance. Find them where your customers already are. Make their job easy: pre-built creative, pre-written copy, clear payout terms, fast attribution.

### Why Lead Getters compound
Doing the Core Four once gets you a lead. Lead Getters do the Core Four *for you*. Lead Getters who recruit *more* Lead Getters compound the system. The endgame: minimal personal effort, maximum lead volume.

## How To Pick The First Channel

Don't try all four at once. Pick based on current constraint:

- **Need clients in the next 7-14 days, no audience yet** → Warm Outreach
- **Long-term audience, not in a rush, want compounding leverage** → Posting Content
- **B2B or high-ticket, can identify specific buyers** → Cold Outreach
- **Have proof, repeatable offer, and budget** → Paid Ads

Then use More-Better-New on whichever channel is producing.

## Operating Principles From The Book

- Engaged leads, not raw leads, are the goal.
- A great lead magnet is a Grand Slam Offer that happens to be free.
- The Core Four is the entire universe of advertising. There is no fifth.
- Volume is the most underused variable. Most lead problems are volume problems.
- Cold needs personalization + big fast value. Warm needs ACA + an easy ask.
- LTGP:CAC ≥ 3:1, with first-30-day payback (CFA), is the unit-economics target.
- Most "ad problems" are business-model problems in disguise.
- The biggest scale unlock is Lead Getters, not Lead Getting.
- Same offer, better wrapper (headline, hook, format) = different result.
- The Rule of 100 settles 90% of "is it working" questions.
